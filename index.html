Abstract

isual imitation learning is a promising approach to training robot manipulation policies capable of completing a wide variety of tasks. However, policies today remain brittle to viewpoint perturbations, making deployment in diverse environments a challenge. In this paper, we explore policy learning approaches that remain performant under such camera viewpoint shifts. We find that viewpoint generalization improves when the visual encoder is geometry-aware and the action head is an equal participant in geometric reasoning. We pair geometry-aware visual features with a relative positional encoding that places visual, proprioception, and action tokens in a shared frame, attending to features in a viewpoint-robust manner. On a suite of simulated tasks spanning a wide range of camera poses, our policy outperforms 2D and 3D baselines, while matching fixed-camera baselines. As a practical consequence, our policy transfers zero-shot from simulation to the real world under random camera configurations.

Keywords: Viewpoint generalization · Visual imitation learning · Sim-to-real

AGP — A Viewpoint-Generalizable Policy

AGP is a Viewpoint Generalizable Manipulation Policy designed to remain performant for all views in the training camera distribution.

AGP distills multi-viewpoint demonstrations into a viewpoint-generalizable visuomotor policy via imitation learning. Given V calibrated RGB views, AGP encodes them with a single transformer initialized from a visual geometry foundation model to produce geometry-aware image tokens. Every image, proprioception, and action token is localized in the robot's base frame with a 3D position and a timestep, and a 4D rotary Diffusion Transformer (DiT) attends over them, trained with a flow-matching objective.

1

Geometry-aware multi-view encoder

The backbone is initialized from Depth Anything 3, which recovers dense 3D geometry from images, so its features encode scene structure across viewpoints. Within-view and cross-view self-attention fuse information across cameras, yielding scene-aware features without 3D input. Critically, we keep the full set of dense tokens — compressing them into a single global vector sharply hurts viewpoint robustness.

2

Robot-frame 4D rotary attention

Each visual, proprioception, and action token gets a 4D coordinate (x, y, z, h) — its 3D position in the robot base frame plus a temporal index — and a 4D RoPE grounds every token in the geometry of the scene. Unlike image-space positional encodings, the geometric relationship between any visual token and the gripper is invariant to the camera pose.

The Cabinet-Opening Task

Open an articulated cabinet from randomly sampled over-the-shoulder views.

We train a policy to imitate an expert motion planner opening a cabinet with a small knob handle from PartNet-Mobility. In contrast to front-facing setups that look toward the robot, we randomly sample two over-the-shoulder camera views per trajectory, plus a wrist-mounted camera that keeps the object visible. We generate 989 demonstration trajectories (100k observation-action pairs) and evaluate on 100 held-out trajectories with freshly sampled camera poses.

What Matters for Viewpoint Generalization?

Three ablations on the Cabinet task isolate the design choices behind AGP — and several are non-obvious from fixed-camera experiments.

Finding 1 Compressing visual information hurts action generation

Many policies squeeze visual observations into a single global vector before the action head. This is fine under a fixed camera, but under randomized cameras the action head loses the spatial information it needs to reason across viewpoints. Keeping all image tokens (no compression) is decisive.

Finding 2 Geometry-aware multi-view encoders generalize better

Finetuning matters, cross-view attention adds further gains, and swapping a DINOv2 backbone for the geometry-grounded Depth Anything 3 encoder — under the same finetuning and cross-view recipe — improves performance from 67 to 70.5, indicating that geometry-aware features are more viewpoint-robust.

Finding 3 Robot-frame positional encoding drives robustness

Image-space positional encodings are fragile under randomized cameras: the same patch index can map to different 3D locations as the camera moves, so the encoding carries no consistent geometric meaning. Grounding every token in the robot frame with 4D RoPE lifts performance from 47.2 to 70.5.

Zero-Shot Sim-to-Real Transfer

A single policy trained entirely in simulation opens real microwaves under camera poses that were never seen during data generation.

We build on Articubot, an automated pipeline for generating demonstrations of a Franka robot opening articulated objects. From its object set we select five microwaves, generate 2,358 demonstrations and over 100,000 state-action pairs, and render them in IsaacLab with extensive domain randomization over microwave colors and textures, the robot, the table, and the background. At deployment, real-world depth is obtained with Fast-FoundationStereo, and the camera poses — unknown during data generation but within the randomized training distribution — are sampled fresh. We report grasp success and normalized opening over 10 trials across 3 camera configurations.

Takeaways

We study viewpoint generalization, relaxing the common constraint of fixed cameras at training and deployment. Our experiments identify two drivers: (1) visual encoders that retain dense scene representations from multi-view backbones encourage viewpoint-robust performance, and (2) grounding every visual, proprioception, and action token in the geometry of the scene matters. We instantiate these recommendations as AGP, a policy that is performant for all views in the camera distribution, matches fixed-camera baselines, and transfers zero-shot from simulation to a real robot under random cameras.

Limitations. AGP requires calibrated RGB-D input at both training and deployment; removing this requirement (estimating extrinsics or operating on monocular RGB with learned depth priors) is an important direction. We also demonstrate sim-to-real as a proof of concept on a single object category; scaling to large, heterogeneous real-world datasets remains open.
